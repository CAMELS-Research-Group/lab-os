# Cohort Retrospective

The [closing ceremony](./index.md) and the [brief presentations](./presentation-worksheet.md) make
the cohort's learnings *visible* to each other. The cohort retrospective goes one step further: it
turns the group's shared experience into **written conventions your team actually adopts** — a habit
added to a `CLAUDE.md`, a rule added to `.claude/rules/`, a decision recorded in `project_log.md`.

It is **optional** and meant for a **live cohort or team**. Solo, self-paced readers produce the
[individual carry-forward artifact](./learnings-carry-forward-worksheet.md) instead — that artifact
travels to *your* next project; the cohort retrospective changes how *the team* works together from
now on.

> **Facilitators:** the full run-of-show — timeboxes, the exact prompts, and a capture template — is
> in the [Cohort Retrospective facilitator runbook](https://github.com/CAMELS-Research-Group/lab-os/blob/main/docs/workshops/closeout/retrospective-facilitator-runbook.md).
> This page is the *why*; the runbook is the *how*.

## When to run it

- You closed the project as a **live cohort or team**, not solo.
- You want the two weeks to change your **shared conventions**, not just each person's private notes.
- You can give it a single **~90-minute session** with the group in one room (or one shared board).

It **complements** the individual movements — it does not replace them. Run it after the individual
carry-forward and presentations, while the shared experience is still fresh.

## What you produce

A short list of **named conventions**, each with:

- a **name** a teammate can say out loud ("read-the-diff", "spec-before-keys"),
- a **one-line rule** a new teammate could follow tomorrow,
- a **home** — where it gets written down so it sticks, and
- an **owner** — who makes that change.

The homes are the same three places this handbook teaches you to keep conventions:

| If the pattern is… | It becomes… | Home |
|---|---|---|
| A rule everyone should follow | a project rule | `.claude/rules/` (see [Rules, explained](../../rules-explained.md)) |
| A fact about how your team works | a working note | a `CLAUDE.md` |
| A one-time decision worth remembering | a log entry | `project_log.md` |

## The shape of the session

The retrospective moves from *everyone's raw experience* to *a few shared conventions*, in four steps:

1. **Reflect** — everyone writes silently first (what you **liked, learned, lacked, and longed for**
   over the two weeks), then merges in pairs and small groups. Writing first means every voice reaches
   the wall, not just the loudest.
2. **Sort** — cluster the notes into **Keep / Drop / Try**: what worked and should continue, what to
   stop, and what new thing to try.
3. **Vote** — each person spends a few dots on the items most worth writing down. This ranks the wall
   so you codify the few that matter, not all of them.
4. **Codify** — for the top items, **name** the convention, write it as **one line**, and decide its
   **home** and **owner**. This is the step that turns a good discussion into a change your team keeps.

The retrospective is done when each top item has a name, a home, and an owner — and someone opens the
change (a pull request adding the rule, the `CLAUDE.md` line, or the `project_log.md` entry).

## Why a separate collective session

The individual carry-forward artifact answers *"what does my next project need to know?"* The cohort
retrospective answers a different question: *"what should our team do differently, together, from now
on?"* Surfacing a shared theme in the presentation round is valuable — but a theme nobody writes down
is one the next cohort rediscovers from scratch. Codifying it is what makes the learning stick.
